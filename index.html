Why Bicolano Recipes Matter

Bicolano cuisine is known for its fearless use of chilies and coconut milk, symbolizing warmth, resilience, and creativity. By learning these dishes, we preserve local culture while embracing flavors that are both comforting and exciting.

This webpage is designed as a simple, student-friendly guide: clear steps, essential ingredients, and quick tips so anyone can confidently cook at home. It’s meant to be a companion for curious food lovers and beginners alike.

Bicol Express

Bicol Express is one of the most popular dishes from the Bicol region, named after the train that once ran from Manila to Bicol. It captures the region’s love for heat and coconut cream, combining pork, chilies, and shrimp paste into a bold, comforting stew.

Ingredients
  • 500 g pork belly, sliced into small strips
  • 2 cups coconut milk
  • 1 cup coconut cream (kakang gata)
  • 5–8 long green chilies (siling haba), sliced
  • 3–5 bird’s eye chilies (siling labuyo), chopped (adjust to taste)
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp shrimp paste (bagoong alamang)
  • 1 tbsp cooking oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
Steps
  1. Heat oil in a pan over medium heat. Sauté garlic and onions until fragrant and translucent.
  2. Add the pork and cook until lightly browned and natural oils are released.
  3. Stir in the shrimp paste and cook for 2–3 minutes to develop flavor.
  4. Pour in the coconut milk and bring to a gentle simmer. Add the green chilies.
  5. Simmer uncovered until the pork is tender and the sauce has reduced slightly.
  6. Add the coconut cream and bird’s eye chilies. Continue simmering until thick and rich.
  7. Season with salt and pepper to taste. Serve hot with steamed rice.
Cooking Tips
  • Adjust the number of chilies based on your heat tolerance. Start mild and add more as needed.
  • Let the coconut milk simmer gently—avoid high heat to prevent curdling.
  • For a lighter version, use leaner pork cuts or substitute with chicken.
Laing

Laing is a classic Bicolano dish made from dried taro leaves simmered slowly in coconut milk and chilies. Traditionally cooked low and slow, it produces a creamy, earthy, and slightly smoky flavor that pairs perfectly with rice.

Ingredients
  • 3 cups dried taro leaves (dahon ng gabi)
  • 3 cups coconut milk
  • 1 cup coconut cream
  • 1 medium onion, sliced
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 thumb-sized ginger, sliced into strips
  • 5–8 green chilies, sliced
  • 3–5 bird’s eye chilies, chopped
  • 2 tbsp dried fish or shrimp (optional)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
Steps
  1. In a pot, combine coconut milk, onion, garlic, ginger, and dried fish or shrimp if using. Bring to a gentle simmer.
  2. Add the green chilies and bird’s eye chilies. Simmer for a few minutes to infuse flavor.
  3. Carefully layer the dried taro leaves on top—do not stir at this point.
  4. Cover and simmer over low heat for 15–20 minutes, allowing the leaves to absorb the coconut milk.
  5. Once softened, gently press the leaves into the sauce. Add coconut cream.
  6. Continue cooking on low heat until thick and oily on top, stirring occasionally.
  7. Season with salt and pepper. Serve with hot rice.
Cooking Tips
  • Do not wash dried taro leaves directly under running water to avoid itchiness; lightly rinse if needed and pat dry.
  • Low heat is key—slow cooking brings out a deeper, richer flavor.
  • You may add pork or dried fish for extra protein and umami.
Pinangat

Pinangat, sometimes called “pinangat na laing,” is a Bicolano specialty from Camalig, Albay. It combines minced meat or fish, chilies, and aromatics wrapped in taro leaves and gently cooked in coconut milk, forming neat, flavorful bundles.

Ingredients
  • 15–20 pieces taro leaves, whole and fresh
  • 300 g ground pork or fish flakes
  • 2 cups coconut milk
  • 1 cup coconut cream
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 5–8 green chilies, whole or sliced
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Kitchen twine or strips of taro stalks for tying (optional)
Steps
  1. In a bowl, combine ground pork or fish with onion, garlic, salt, and pepper. Mix well.
  2. Lay a taro leaf flat and place a spoonful of filling in the center. Add a slice of chili if desired.
  3. Fold the sides and roll into a neat parcel. Use additional leaves if needed for thickness.
  4. Arrange the parcels in a pot, seam side down. Pour in the coconut milk and add remaining chilies.
  5. Simmer gently over low heat until the filling is cooked and the sauce reduces.
  6. Pour in the coconut cream and continue cooking until thick and slightly oily.
  7. Serve the parcels with sauce spooned over hot rice.
Cooking Tips
  • Use young taro leaves if possible for a more tender texture.
  • Do not boil vigorously—gentle simmering keeps the parcels intact.
  • You can stack multiple parcels; just ensure all are partially submerged in coconut milk.
Kinalas

Kinalas is a beloved noodle dish from Naga City, featuring tender shaved meat from a slow-cooked head or bone, served over noodles in a rich, savory broth. It’s usually topped with gravy, scallions, and sometimes a soft-boiled egg.

Ingredients
  • 500 g beef or pork bones with meat (for broth)
  • 300 g egg noodles or miki noodles
  • 1 medium onion, quartered
  • 4 cloves garlic, crushed
  • 1 bay leaf
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil
  • 2 tbsp flour (for gravy)
  • 2 cups broth (for gravy)
  • Spring onions, chopped (garnish)
  • Optional: boiled egg, toasted garlic, calamansi
Steps
  1. In a large pot, simmer the bones with onion, garlic, bay leaf, salt, and pepper until meat is very tender and broth is flavorful.
  2. Remove meat from the bones and shred or “shave” into small pieces. Set aside. Strain the broth.
  3. Cook the noodles according to package instructions. Drain and set aside.
  4. For the gravy, heat oil in a pan, add flour, and cook until lightly browned. Slowly whisk in broth until smooth and slightly thickened.
  5. To assemble, place noodles in a bowl, pour hot broth, top with shaved meat and gravy.
  6. Garnish with spring onions, toasted garlic, and boiled egg if desired. Serve with calamansi on the side.
Cooking Tips
  • Long, slow simmering builds depth of flavor in the broth—don’t rush this step.
  • Use a mix of bones with marrow for a richer, silkier soup.
  • Adjust the thickness of the gravy based on preference by adding more broth or flour.
